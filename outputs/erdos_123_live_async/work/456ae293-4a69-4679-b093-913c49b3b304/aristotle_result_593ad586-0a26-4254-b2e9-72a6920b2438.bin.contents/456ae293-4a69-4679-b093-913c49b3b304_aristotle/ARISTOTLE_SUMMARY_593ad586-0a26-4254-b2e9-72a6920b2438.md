### Blocker

Pairwise coprimality is **essential**. Without it, the generators can share common prime factors, causing the multiplicative semigroup to collapse toward a chain under divisibility. In the extreme case `a = b = c = 2`, the semigroup is just `{2^n}` — a total chain — so every antichain is a singleton and only powers of 2 are representable as antichain-sums.

### Discovery: Which Special Cases Transfer?

The obstruction is best reformulated in terms of the **divisibility dimension** of the semigroup:

- **Dimension 1 (all generators are powers of a single base):** The semigroup is a chain ⇒ d-completeness fails. This is the counterexample.
- **Dimension ≥ 2 (generators are multiplicatively independent):** The semigroup contains rich antichains. Coprime pairs `{a^i · b^j}` with `gcd(a,b) = 1` are the simplest solved case — d-completeness follows from classical results on smooth number representations. This transfers to any triple where at least two generators are coprime (giving a 2D antichain supply), which is strictly weaker than full pairwise coprimality.
- **The original Erdős Problem 123** (full pairwise coprimality, dimension 3) remains an open problem even in the coprime case.

The correct generalization replaces "pairwise coprime" with "the generators are not all powers of a single integer" (i.e., divisibility dimension ≥ 2).
